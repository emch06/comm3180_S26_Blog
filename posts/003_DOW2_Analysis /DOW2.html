Is extroversion related to race?

I have always heard the saying whether from my parents, friends, or from my own observations that some races are more outgoing than others. This is a topic heavily discussed in media and psychology as well, which is the fact that based on your race (more commonly dicussed in the cultural upbringing context) you are described to be more expressive and social. I have frequently heard about the the generalizations of Asians being more introverted than other Europeans, which to a certain extent, I always thought of as true. This is all very much an interesting topic that can ignite intense conversation at parties, but how truly is this accurate when analyzing real personality data?

Why is this question even important?

This question feels especially relevant to me given that it is something I also noticed in my life, where I assumed extroversion was linked to race. Many of my Asian friends are introverts, and my Caucasian friends all relatively extroverted. I wanted to delve more into this topic, especially because extroversion is often discussed in the media as being connected to cultural upbringing. I wanted to explore whether data could back this up. Based on self-reported levels of extroversion in different racial groups in the Open Psychometrics Big Five dataset, I sought to observe any noticeable patterns that could explain potential associations.

Defining extroversion in this big data set:

To actually start finding the answer to the question, I have to find the extroversion score for each user. To find extroversion from the entire test, I summed the responses (numerical values 1-being strong no, 2-being strong yes) of these five questions in the 49 question set that described extroverted qualities in the best capacity: - E1: “I am the life of the party” - E3: “I feel comfortable around peoplea - E5:”I start conversations” - E7: “I talk to a lot of different people at parties” - E9: “I don’t mind being the center of attention”

Each numerical value for the questions were added to make a total that defined each user’s Extroversion Value:

big5_df["E_value"] = big5_df[["E1", "E3", "E5", "E7", "E9" ]].sum(axis=1)

This would create a seperate column in the compiled chart that gave each user an Extroversion score or E_value

Now I had to find the important stuff which was association. This was found by grouping each user by race, and then averaging the Extroversion scores (E_value) to calculate the total average extroversion score for each race:

group = big5_df.groupby("race_cat") 
race_avg = group["E_value"].mean()

Results:

As seen in the graph, most racial groups cluster between 15-16 on the extroversion scale. At first glance, Artic shows overall higher averages, but it does not reveal why which is what I uncover later on in this analysis. Overall, the differences between each racial group and their extroversion score was only around 1.39 on a 20 point scale, which indicates that there is not a strong association or noticeable pattern that can indicate that there is correlation between extroversion and race.

There is no association between extroversion and race based on this dataset.

What stood out from this initial finding is that there is diversity in the dataset, but it is not proportionally represented across all races, which definitely dilutes my results. The European demographic responses dominate overwhemingly (more than half of the responses) which creates an inaccurate representation of the extroversion score of other races that are not European. There is an imbalance in representation. A problem that stood out to me.

And if we look even closer…

It showes that for artic, only 14 people identified in that racial group, which explains why there average extroversion score is 19 when others are in the 15-16 range. Having a smaller sample size can also inflate certain averages.

Realization:

I realized that my finding (no relation between race and extroversion) cannot be generalized. My finding is weak as my data set had a lot of limitations, which hindered an accurate and true representation of races and their average extroversion score. It fails to reflect actual extroversion levels. So this leaves the very discussed question of whether race is related to extroversion open for anyone else to solve.
